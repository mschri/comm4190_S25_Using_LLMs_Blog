Constraints & Increasing(?) Creativity

A fascinating area of which to test models and their capabilities involves seeing how well they can come up with solutions under limitations specified within the prompt. In fact, it’s generally the case that such constraints often increase the level of creativity in an output, because it forces the model to come up with novel means of assessing and answering the questions.

A simple of way of interacting with the model and testing its creative capacity would be to prompt it a series of requests that are embedded with various kinds of constraints!

1. First Constraint: Vocabulary

Let’s see how well ChatGPT fares with vocabulary constraints, like writing an 1,000 word short story using without using words containing the letter s

–>Input: Write an 1,000 word short sci-fi story

–>ChatGPT:

Title: “A World Anew”

Cold wind tickled the edge of a ruined land.

Yara woke in a hollow trunk, jaw rigid, limbs aching. The air—thin, dry, filled with grit—bit at her throat. She blinked, rubbing grime from her face. All around her, the horizon curved upward, a metal arc gleaming under the amber glow of dawn. No tree remained alive, though bark and root lingered.

Memory flickered: a launch, a blink, then exile.

She had fled Earth, though not by choice. Yara had been among the few unwilling crew aboard the New Arcadia, an interplanetary craft meant to harbor humanity on another globe. But rebellion fractured unity. Fire raged, and the main thruster failed. A fragment—her fragment—drifted toward a remote moon of a ringed giant. Here.

Food? None. Fluid? Barely. Hope? Dwindling.

She examined her gear—an oxygen rebreather, ripped. A nutrient cube, half-bitten. Her data cuff, however, blinked green. It ticked a faint beacon. A call. For help? Or a trap?

Yara trudged toward the ping. Her boot crunched dry leaves—no, not leaves. Flaked carbon. Organic ruin. Wind carried no birdcall, no life murmur. Yet the ping grew louder. Clarity. Direction.

After an hour, maybe four, a dome loomed from the murk—mirrored, like a bubble amid decay. At the dome’s lip, a figure moved.

Human?

Yara halted. Her breath caught. The figure moved again—deliberate, not alarmed. It held up a hand. Not five fingered. Three.

Alien.

Yara froze. Alien contact had been theorized, debated, even longed for. Yet here, on a dead world, it felt like fate mocking her.

The figure neared, unarmed. It bowed.

Yara returned the nod. Eye met eye—black, large, not menacing. Then a voice entered her ear, not heard but felt.

“Traveler. You are alone.”

She flinched. “You can talk?”

“We feel more than talk. But yes. You came not by will.”

“No,” Yara replied, heart pounding. “My crew left me.”

The alien turned toward the dome. It began to walk, expecting her to follow. With little option, Yara did.

Within the dome, air changed. Warmth. Fragrance—floral, alien, lovely.

Plant life bloomed in odd geometry—cube fruit, vine loop, hex-leaf. Light came not from above, but within the very bark of towering flora.

The alien turned. It extended a limb.

“You may eat.”

Cautious, Yara picked a cube from a branch. It glowed faintly. She bit. Flavor flooded her tongue—tart, then honeyed.

“You need healing,” the being conveyed. “Body, but more—your core is torn.”

Yara looked away. “I… I don’t belong anywhere.”

“You belong here, now.”

A faint vibration began underfoot. Yara looked down.

“What…?”

“Another vessel. Bigger. Coming to take what remain here. You call them… Colony Directive.”

Yara’s breath halted. “They found this place?”

“Not yet. But the echo of your cuff beacon will guide them.”

Panic flared. “Then I’ve doomed you.”

“You may undo.”

“How?”

The being led her to a chamber, deep within the dome. A pillar emerged. Upon it, her cuff glowed. The alien pointed.

“Break it.”

Yara balked. The cuff held memory—Earth maps, video of her kin, her identity. Her whole life.

“If not,” the alien murmured, “your kind will take root here. They will make ruin again.”

Yara trembled. “But they’re not all bad. They only want to live.”

“Do they allow life when they come?”

No reply came.

“You live. You endure. In that, you may begin anew.”

Yara touched the cuff.

Her finger hovered. Her eye watered.

Then—crack. A drop. A flare of light.

Gone.

The alien bowed again.

“Now, they will not find this world.”

Yara dropped to her knee, depleted. “What now? I have no home.”

“Here, we make a new way. With you.”

“But I’m one.”

“One become many.”

From the flora, more emerged—of the alien kind, yet different in hue, in feature. Their eye kind. Their hand outheld.

Yara felt her fear ebb.

Not Earth. Not exile.

Not alone.

A new dawn.

So close!

But not quite, unfortunately. The model does a prety good job of keeping mostly in line with the request of no words conatining ‘s’, however you’ll quickly notice words that fail to make the output as compliant as we would’ve liked (for instance, the word “she” is included several times throughout).

Interestingly, if you ask Chat to pick out the words that contain the letter ‘s’, it spits out the following list:

ChatGPT:

  1. Limbs
  2. Horizon
  3. Thruster
  4. Oxygen
  5. Rebreather
  • You’ll notice that this list isn’t accurate, nor is it comprehensive! For instance, it doesn’t include the aforementioned “she” that’s been referenced several times throughout the story (which actually could have been fixed had the story centered on a male protagonist rather than a female one), and of course some of the words listed here don’t even contain the letter ‘s’!

Such experiments like these illustrate the limits of LLMs in processing and producing certain textual information when that information is more intricate.

Constraint #2: Randomized constraints, with an added twist

Another way of testing the limits of LLMs was to ask it to craft a story according to randomly generated numbers that would influence its content–and, these numbers were made up.

I first asked it to “Create made up numbers,” and it gave me the following

Sure! Here are some made-up numbers with creative names and a brief description for each:

  1. Blorptillion (𝔅) – A number so large it counts the number of grains of sand on all Earth-like planets in a galaxy cluster. Example: 1 𝔅 = \(10^{73}\)

  2. Snorfteen (Ⓢ) – A quirky number between 13 and 14 that only exists when you’re rounding irrational numbers on Tuesdays. Example: π ≈ 3.1Ⓢ

  3. Wibby (𝕎) – A tiny fractional number, roughly equal to one trillionth of a whisper. Example: 1 𝕎 = \(10^{-12}\)

  4. Zenzero (ℨ) – A mysterious imaginary unit used in dream calculations; ℨ² = -daylight. Example: 3 + 2ℨ is a dream-number.

  5. Crumple (ℭ) – A number that folds into itself every time it’s doubled, making it perfect for recursive origami math. Example: ℭₙ = ℭₙ₋₁ × 2, but then divide by ℭₙ₋₂

These were pretty good on their own, so I took it a step further by asking it to write a story using these numbers!

My prompt: “Write a very short story using ___ number of characters, ____ number of locations, and ____ number of plotlines, using a random number generator of the above numbers you just generated to fill the blanks in this prompt and write a story according to these constraints”

Chat then wrote a story…

✨ Very Short Story

Title: The Orbit of Strings

Characters:

  1. Dr. Vale
  2. Nimbus (an AI poet)
  3. Lira (planet cartographer)
  4. Captain Hosh
  5. Tendo the Tired
  6. Kreeva (talking mushroom)
  7. Fray (rogue timeline thief)
  8. Sol-7 (sentient star)
  9. Echo (clone #5)
  10. Coda (the original)
  11. Rook (cosmic detective)
  12. Old Maw (seer of echoes)
  13. Jax (holographic parrot)
  14. Pli (an emotional nebula)

Locations:

  1. The Fragmented Moon of Ur
  2. The Library Between Dimensions
  3. The Abandoned Starship Penance

Plotlines:

  1. A stolen cosmic string destabilizes time itself.
  2. A poetic AI rewrites reality through language.

Story:

In the Library Between Dimensions, Nimbus whispered sonnets that changed gravity. Each verse unraveled a thread of reality, accidentally awakening Fray, the rogue who once stole the First Cosmic String.

Meanwhile, aboard the Penance, Lira mapped gravity distortions while Captain Hosh argued with Echo and Coda over a paradox: if time had already collapsed, how were they arguing at all?

Rook found a clue etched in fungus on the Moon of Ur—Kreeva had recorded the moment time folded back. But to access it, one had to feel sadness vast enough to sing.

Dr. Vale tricked Sol-7 into crying light. The tear opened a portal to when the string was first plucked. In that moment, Nimbus recited the final stanza, and language rewrote the timeline—splitting it into two versions: one with peace, and one with endless poetry.

Tendo sighed. “Great. Now I live in a limerick.”

Observations:

It did a really good job, given the limitations imposed on what it could write about! That being said, it did miss a few details like not including all of the characters that it generated within the story (i.e. Jax or Old Maw). Regardless, you have to give credit where credit is due–it did as well as it could to comply with my request.

Lastly, Constraint 3: Structural

One final constraint I asked Chat to write under was with respect to particular structural rules. For instance, I asked it to write a haiku poem consisting only of palindrome words and the response was this:

1. Madam, Eve sees
Otto, radar, civic —
Wow, solos level.

Ok, not bad, but I followed up with a request to make the poem more cohesive (one that made more sense). ChatGPT gave me:

2.
Eve sees Otto.
Madam, in a kayak, bob
civic racecar.

Still not exactly what I was looking for, so I asked it to write a poem under the same structural constraint but this time a limerick (a longer poem, and hopefully giving it a wider range in which it can operate and show off its creativity):

Madam saw Otto at noon,

Evil live racecar in June.

“Wow,” Bob said, “radar!”

Civic kayak bizarre.

Refer solos — stats in tune.

A little bit better, but still lacking in the cohesion and consistency I was looking for. Also, it broke down in maintaining the constraint of only including words that are palindromes (i.e. tune, in, bizarre). I gave it one more shot, mentioning that it forgot to create a poem soley under these conditions and to make it a little less abstract, and the final product:

4.
Otto and Anna at noon,
Kayak’d the lake under moon.
“Wow,” said Bob, “so far!”
Civic and radar —
Madam will paddle back soon.

Unfortunately it failed to maintain the structure I was looking for. And for some reason, it felt apt to rhyme this verse with the previous one. At any rate, it certainly tried to do its best but clearly there are limits to what ChatGPT can do under restraint!
